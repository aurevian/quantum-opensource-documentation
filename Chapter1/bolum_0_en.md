One of the clearest ways to explain quantum machine learning systematically is to classify problems according to **which world the input comes from** and **which world the output belongs to**. This approach provides a conceptually orderly and understandable framework for organizing quantum machine learning problems.

In this respect, two main axes stand out:

The first is whether the data comes from the **classical** or the **quantum** world; the second is whether the output is **classical** or **quantum** in nature.

## a. Input (Data) Type

- **CC (Classical–Classical Data):** Data represented on a classical computer, such as vectors, images, tables, or time series.

- **QQ (Quantum–Quantum Data):** Data represented by quantum states or quantum channels, such as experimentally prepared quantum states, multi-qubit density matrices, or states produced by a quantum circuit.

## b. Output Type

- **QC (Quantum $\rightarrow$ Classical output):** A quantum computation is performed, but the final product is a classical number or label, typically after measurement and classical post-processing. Examples include a classification label, regression value, or probability.

- **QQ (Quantum $\rightarrow$ Quantum output):** The final product is a quantum state or quantum channel. In other words, the output remains quantum so that it can serve as input to another quantum algorithm.

Combining these two axes yields four fundamental problem classes:

$$
CC \rightarrow QC,\; CC \rightarrow QQ,\; QQ \rightarrow QC,\; QQ \rightarrow QQ.
$$

Below, we discuss each of these classes in a scientific yet accessible way.

---

#### 1.1) CC $\rightarrow$ QC: Computing with Classical Data / Producing Classical Predictions

This class is currently the most widespread part of the practical quantum machine learning (QML) literature: *data arrives in classical form, is processed within a quantum circuit, and a classical output is obtained through measurement.*

The typical workflow is as follows:

1. **Classical data encoding (encoding / feature map):**

   - An input such as $x \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is embedded into a quantum state by means of a quantum circuit whose parameters depend on $x$:
     $$
     x \mapsto \lvert \phi(x) \rangle = U_{\phi}(x)\, \lvert 0 \rangle .
     $$

2. **Quantum processing (ansatz / kernel / evolution):**

   - Another circuit is applied to this state, for example a parameterized ansatz.

3. **Measurement and classical readout:**

   - Observables such as Pauli expectation values or probabilities are converted into classical numbers:
     $$
     y = f\big( \langle O \rangle_{\lvert \psi(x) \rangle} \big).
     $$

4. **Learning:**

   - A classical loss function is minimized, such as cross-entropy or MSE.

Within this class, the following problems can be defined:

- **Classification:** Extracting class probabilities or labels from the measurement outcomes of a quantum circuit.

- **Regression:** Predicting continuous outputs from expectation values.

- **Representation learning:** Using measurements generated by a quantum feature map as a kind of feature vector.

- **Physics/chemistry targets:** Predicting energies or properties from classical inputs such as molecular descriptors; the output is typically a classical number.

A scientifically critical point to note here is that evaluating the success of such models requires several additional criteria:

1. The expressive power of the chosen encoding,

2. The sampling cost of measurement statistics (shot noise),

3. Optimization difficulty (barren plateaus, noise).

---

#### 1.2) CC $\rightarrow$ QQ: Producing Quantum States from Classical Data / Generating Quantum Outputs

In this class, the data is classical, but the goal is to produce a quantum state. That is, the model output is not a label or a number, but directly a quantum object:

$$
x \mapsto \rho(x) \quad \text{or} \quad x \mapsto \lvert \psi(x) \rangle .
$$

This setting arises from two main motivations:

1. **Quantum data generation / quantum generative modeling**

   - The goal is to represent a distribution as a quantum state or to learn a family of quantum states.  
     Example: a circuit whose parameters are derived from classical data prepares the quantum state corresponding to that data.

2. **Input preparation for quantum algorithms (state preparation)**

   - Some quantum algorithms, such as simulation routines, HHL-like linear algebra procedures, or amplitude encoding schemes, require a quantum state directly.  
     Therefore, the learning problem may become the task of finding a circuit that encodes classical data into a quantum state with a desired level of accuracy.

Within this class, the following problems can be defined:

- Generative modeling (quantum state generation),

- Representation learning: transforming classical data into a more suitable representation in quantum space; since the output remains quantum, it can be chained with later quantum modules.

A scientifically critical point here is that **accuracy** is often not the primary evaluation criterion. Instead, one typically uses:

- Fidelity,

- Trace distance,

- Observable matching,

- Kernel alignment.

---

#### 1.3) QQ $\rightarrow$ QC: Extracting Classical Decisions or Numbers from Quantum Data (measure, classify, estimate)

In this class, the input is quantum: one has a family of quantum states or a quantum channel. The goal is to extract classical information from it. The most natural example is **quantum state discrimination**. In general, this can be written mathematically as:

$$
\rho \;\mapsto\; y \in \mathbb{R}
\quad \text{or} \quad
\rho \;\mapsto\; y \in \{1,\dots,K\}.
$$

At this point, one may ask: **Why is this a separate class?** Because there is no data-loading problem here; the data already exists on a quantum device or in quantum memory. The task of the model is to extract information from that state using suitable measurement strategies.

In this sense, typical applications include:

- **Classification:** Determining which class a given quantum state belongs to (state discrimination),

- **Regression / parameter estimation:** Estimating a physical parameter from quantum sensor data,

- **Phase recognition:** Extracting classical phase labels from quantum many-body systems,

- **Experimental noise diagnosis:** Learning error or noise parameters from the outputs of quantum devices.

The scientifically critical point in this model is somewhat more detailed. Here, the limiting factor is not only **model capacity**, but also **measurement design**. When quantum data is converted into classical information through measurement, information loss may occur. Therefore, the following concepts become important:

- POVM design,

- Adaptive measurements,

- Sampling cost,

- Statistical limits (for example, the Helstrom bound).

---

#### 1.4) QQ $\rightarrow$ QQ: Transforming Quantum Data into Quantum Form

In this class, both the input and the output are quantum:

$$
\rho_{\text{in}} \;\mapsto\; \rho_{\text{out}}.
$$

This can be viewed as a **learned quantum channel** or a **learned quantum circuit transformation**.

At this point, one may ask: **Why is this important?** Because here QML is embedded directly into quantum information processing tasks rather than merely producing classical labels. Tasks such as compression, error correction, entanglement restructuring, state transformation, and channel learning fall into this framework.

Typical applications include:

- **Representation learning / compression:** Quantum autoencoders that aim to discard irrelevant subspaces while preserving the information-bearing one,

- **Error mitigation / learning-assisted error correction:** Learned quantum transformations that compensate for noise effects,

- **Quantum circuit compilation / optimization:** Learning a circuit that approximates a target unitary,

- **Physics/chemistry simulation:** Learned channels that approximately implement the evolution of a quantum system.

A scientifically critical point here is that in QQ $\rightarrow$ QQ models, the objective functions differ from standard classical losses; one needs quantum-specific targets such as fidelity, channel distances, or observable agreement. In addition, verification can be costly, because characterizing a quantum output often requires full tomography, which leads to scalability challenges.

---

#### 1.5) How Do Application Classes Fit into This Typology?

Let us now relate the application classes we mentioned earlier—classification, regression, generative modeling, representation learning, and physics/chemistry targets—to this four-part typology.

The key idea is this: the application label answers the question **“What do we want to do?”**, whereas the CC/QC/QQ distinction answers the question **“What do we start with, and what do we produce?”** The same application may appear under different input/output types.

##### 1. Classification

- **CC $\rightarrow$ QC:** The most common case; classical data, quantum circuit, class label obtained from measurement.

- **QQ $\rightarrow$ QC:** Quantum state classification (state discrimination, phase recognition).

##### 2. Regression

- **CC $\rightarrow$ QC:** Continuous prediction from expectation values.

- **QQ $\rightarrow$ QC:** Parameter estimation from quantum sensor readings.

##### 3. Generative Modeling

- **CC $\rightarrow$ QQ:** Quantum state generation conditioned on classical input (quantum conditional generation).

- **QQ $\rightarrow$ QQ:** Quantum channels that transform or generate quantum distributions; for example, transformations that map states from a quantum source into a target distribution.

##### 4. Representation Learning

- **CC $\rightarrow$ QC:** Using quantum measurements as features and learning representations in classical space.

- **CC $\rightarrow$ QQ:** The representation remains in quantum space and is passed to later quantum modules.

- **QQ $\rightarrow$ QQ:** Learning quantum representations directly, as in quantum autoencoders.

##### 5. Physics/Chemistry Targets

This domain can span all four classes:

- **CC $\rightarrow$ QC:** Predicting energy or properties from molecular descriptors,

- **CC $\rightarrow$ QQ:** Preparing a target quantum state from classical parameters,

- **QQ $\rightarrow$ QC:** Extracting phases or physical parameters from experimental quantum systems,

- **QQ $\rightarrow$ QQ:** Learning a simulation or evolution operator, or improving physical accuracy through error mitigation.

Understanding these distinctions is important because they are not merely terminology; they also indicate in advance **what type of bottleneck or cost we are likely to face**. For example:

- **CC $\rightarrow$ QC:** The bottleneck is usually the encoding choice, shot cost, and optimization.

- **CC $\rightarrow$ QQ:** The bottleneck is output evaluation (e.g., fidelity) and the complexity of state preparation.

- **QQ $\rightarrow$ QC:** The bottleneck is measurement design and the limits of information extraction.

- **QQ $\rightarrow$ QQ:** The bottleneck is verification cost, state/channel metrics, and scalable training.

Therefore, when designing a QML project, the question **“Which class am I in?”** is decisive for experimental design, resource budgeting (shots, number of qubits), verification protocol, and publication-grade methodology.
